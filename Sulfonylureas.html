Sulfonylurea Toxicity

"One Pill Can Kill" Warning: Medications like Glipizide, Glyburide, and Glimepiride stimulate insulin release from the pancreas independent of blood glucose levels. A single pill can cause severe, prolonged, and recurrent hypoglycemia in a child lasting up to 24-72 hours.

Clinical Presentation

  • Vitals: Tachycardia (sympathetic response to hypoglycemia).
  • Symptoms: Diaphoresis, pallor, tremors, hunger.
  • Neuro: Lethargy, confusion, coma, seizures, permanent neurologic damage if untreated.

Weight-Based Resuscitation & Antidotes

1. Dextrose Bolus

Restores immediate blood glucose. Avoid massive boluses if possible, as this stimulates the pancreas to release even *more* insulin, creating a rebound hypoglycemia cycle.

  • Pediatric Dose (0.5 - 1 g/kg):
  • Neonates/Infants: D10W at 5-10 mL/kg IV.
  • Children: D25W at 2-4 mL/kg IV.
  • Adolescents/Adults: D50W at 1-2 mL/kg IV (or 1 ampule).

2. Dextrose Infusion

Maintenance fluid to keep blood glucose stable after the initial bolus.

  • Start fluids containing D5W or D10W at 1.5x maintenance rate.
  • Frequent blood glucose checks (every 1-2 hours) are mandatory.

3. Octreotide

The definitive antidote. A somatostatin analog that directly blocks the release of insulin from the pancreatic beta cells, stopping the hyperinsulinemic cycle.

  • Pediatric Dose: 1 - 2 mcg/kg IV or Subcutaneous (SC) every 6 hours.
  • Max Single Dose: 50 mcg.
  • Adult Dose: 50 - 100 mcg IV or SC every 6 hours.
  • Note: Indicated if a patient requires more than one dextrose bolus or needs a continuous dextrose infusion to maintain euglycemia. Monitor for 24 hours minimum after the last dose.
